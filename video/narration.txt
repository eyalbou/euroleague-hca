Do EuroLeague home teams win more? And if they do, why?

I analyzed ten seasons of EuroLeague basketball. Twenty-eight hundred games. One point one million play-by-play events. All of it running locally, in Cursor, on my laptop.

One dataset. Two investigations. Track A -- why home wins. Track B -- how possessions chain. Let's start with Track A.

The headline. EuroLeague home teams win one point seven five times more often than road teams. Plus three point eight eight points per game, on average. A ten-season mean, stable across seasons.

So the question becomes: why?

The old answer, from N B A research, is referee bias. Home refs whistle fewer fouls on home players. I tested it for Europe. Sixty one referees, each with at least thirty games.

After Holm correction for sixty one simultaneous tests, the number of statistically biased referees came out to zero. European officiating, within our statistical power, is neutral. That hypothesis is dead.

So what is driving the home edge? I decomposed every box-score mechanism. Shooting efficiency. Turnovers. Free-throw rate. Pace.

Then, COVID became a natural experiment nobody could run on purpose. Games played in empty arenas dropped home advantage by almost two points per game. When fans came back, so did the edge. Crowd presence matters -- but it is one layer, not the whole story.

The full model explains ninety four percent of home advantage from measurable, in-game mechanism.

Okay. Mechanism. But where does that plus three point eight eight actually live, play by play? For that: Track B.

After every action on the floor, what comes next? First-order Markov chains, computed over one point one million events.

Then I added a home-versus-road lens on top. Home teams score zero point zero four nine more points per possession than road teams. That tiny per-possession edge, times eighty possessions a game, reproduces the entire three point eight eight.

Every action. Nineteen of nineteen possession starters. The home edge is not one big thing. It is a thousand small things.

Two-step chains -- what I call Storylines. After a home-team defensive rebound, the next three events cascade, slightly, in the home team's favor. Momentum exists. It is just micro.

Two more things fell out of this analysis.

After a three-point miss, offensive-rebound odds are measurably higher than after a two-point miss. After a terminal free-throw miss, different again. Three shooting regimes, three rebound dynamics -- with confidence intervals on every number.

And a catalog of ten basketball anomalies: the first-score effect, tied-at-half home-win rates, quarter-by-quarter asymmetry -- each with a basketball-analyst interpretation.
